Differentiation: Definition & Basic Rules

1. What is a derivative?

The derivative \(f'(x)\) measures the instantaneous rate of change of \(f\) — the slope of the line tangent to the curve at a point. It is defined as a limit:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$

That fraction is the slope between two nearby points; letting \(h \to 0\) slides them together into a single tangent point.

2. The power rule and friends

You rarely use the limit definition in practice — these shortcuts do the work:

  • Power rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,x^n = n\,x^{n-1}\)
  • Constant: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,c = 0\)
  • Constant multiple: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,[c\,f(x)] = c\,f'(x)\)
  • Sum / difference: differentiate term by term

3. Derivatives worth memorizing

  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,e^x = e^x\)
  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\ln x = \dfrac{1}{x}\)
  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\sin x = \cos x\)
  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\cos x = -\sin x\)

4. Product & quotient rules

When functions are multiplied or divided, you can't just differentiate each piece:

$$\textbf{Product: }\;(fg)' = f'g + fg'$$

$$\textbf{Quotient: }\;\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}$$

Step-by-step solutions

Click any example to expand the full solution.

▶Example 1 — Derivative from the limit definition: \(f(x) = x^2\)
  1. Set up the definition: \(f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0}\dfrac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h}\).
  2. Expand the top: \((x+h)^2 - x^2 = x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2 = 2xh + h^2\).
  3. Divide by \(h\): \(\dfrac{2xh + h^2}{h} = 2x + h\).
  4. Let \(h \to 0\): \(2x + 0\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{f'(x) = 2x}\) — which matches the power rule.
▶Example 2 — Power rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,(3x^4 - 5x^2 + 7)\)
  1. Differentiate term by term.
  2. \(3x^4 \to 3 \cdot 4x^3 = 12x^3\).
  3. \(-5x^2 \to -5 \cdot 2x = -10x\).
  4. \(7 \to 0\) (constant).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{12x^3 - 10x}\)
▶Example 3 — Product rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,(x^2 \sin x)\)
  1. Let \(f = x^2\) and \(g = \sin x\), so \(f' = 2x\) and \(g' = \cos x\).
  2. Apply \((fg)' = f'g + fg'\): \((2x)(\sin x) + (x^2)(\cos x)\).
  3. Answer: \(\;\boxed{2x\sin x + x^2\cos x}\)
▶Example 4 — Quotient rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\dfrac{x^2}{x+1}\)
  1. Let \(f = x^2\) (\(f' = 2x\)) and \(g = x+1\) (\(g' = 1\)).
  2. Apply \(\dfrac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}\): \(\dfrac{(2x)(x+1) - (x^2)(1)}{(x+1)^2}\).
  3. Expand the top: \(2x^2 + 2x - x^2 = x^2 + 2x\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{x^2 + 2x}{(x+1)^2}}\)
▶Example 5 — The derivative as a slope (visual): tangent to \(f(x) = x^2\) at \(x = 1\)
  1. \(f'(x) = 2x\), so the slope at \(x = 1\) is \(f'(1) = 2\).
  2. The point on the curve is \((1, f(1)) = (1, 1)\).
  3. Tangent line: \(y - 1 = 2(x - 1)\), i.e. \(y = 2x - 1\).

The tangent line just grazes the parabola at \((1, 1)\) with slope 2 — that's exactly what the derivative measures.
