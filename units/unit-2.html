Differentiation: Definition & Basic Rules

The derivative is the rate of change — the slope of a curve at a single instant. This unit builds it from the limit definition, then hands you the shortcut rules (power, product, quotient) you'll use for the rest of the course.

1. What is a derivative?

The derivative \(f'(x)\) measures the instantaneous rate of change of \(f\) — the slope of the line tangent to the curve at a point. It's defined as a limit of slopes of secant lines:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$

The fraction is the slope between two nearby points; letting \(h \to 0\) slides them together into a single tangent point. (An equivalent form fixes the point \(a\): \(f'(a) = \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\).)

2. Notation & the derivative as a function

The same idea has several notations — get comfortable with all of them:

$$f'(x) \;=\; \frac{dy}{dx} \;=\; \frac{d}{dx}\big[f(x)\big] \;=\; y'$$

The derivative is itself a function: plug in any \(x\) and it returns the slope there. Evaluating it at one point, like \(f'(2)\), gives a single number — the slope at \(x = 2\).

3. Differentiability vs. continuity

If \(f\) is differentiable at a point, it must be continuous there. The reverse is not true — a function can be continuous but have no derivative where the graph has a:

  • corner or cusp (like \(|x|\) at \(0\) — the slope jumps),
  • vertical tangent (slope is infinite),
  • or a discontinuity (no slope at a break).

Slogan: differentiable ⟹ continuous, but continuous ⟹̸ differentiable.

4. The power rule & combinations

You rarely use the limit definition in practice — these shortcuts do the work:

  • Power rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,x^n = n\,x^{n-1}\) (works for any real \(n\) — negatives and fractions too)
  • Constant: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,c = 0\)
  • Constant multiple: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,[c\,f(x)] = c\,f'(x)\)
  • Sum / difference: differentiate term by term

The trick most students miss: rewrite roots and fractions as powers first — \(\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}\), \(\dfrac{1}{x^2} = x^{-2}\) — then apply the power rule.

5. Derivatives worth memorizing

  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,e^x = e^x\)
  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\ln x = \dfrac{1}{x}\)
  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\sin x = \cos x\),   \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\cos x = -\sin x\)
  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\tan x = \sec^2 x\)

6. The product rule

When two functions are multiplied, you can't just multiply their derivatives:

$$(fg)' = f'g + fg'$$

"Derivative of the first times the second, plus the first times the derivative of the second."

7. The quotient rule

For a fraction of two functions:

$$\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}$$

Order matters because of the subtraction — memorize "low d-high minus high d-low, over low squared."

8. Higher-order derivatives

Differentiate again and you get the second derivative \(f''(x)\) (the rate of change of the slope — curvature), then \(f'''(x)\), and so on. In motion problems, position → velocity → acceleration is just differentiating twice.

▶Example 1 — Derivative from the limit definition: \(f(x) = x^2\)
  1. Set up the definition: \(f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0}\dfrac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h}\).
  2. Expand the top: \((x+h)^2 - x^2 = x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2 = 2xh + h^2\).
  3. Divide by \(h\): \(\dfrac{2xh + h^2}{h} = 2x + h\).
  4. Let \(h \to 0\): \(2x + 0\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{f'(x) = 2x}\) — which matches the power rule.
▶Example 2 — Limit definition with a fraction: \(f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}\)
  1. Definition: \(f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0}\dfrac{\frac{1}{x+h} - \frac{1}{x}}{h}\).
  2. Combine the top over \(x(x+h)\): \(\dfrac{x - (x+h)}{x(x+h)} = \dfrac{-h}{x(x+h)}\).
  3. Divide by \(h\): \(\dfrac{-h}{x(x+h)}\cdot\dfrac{1}{h} = \dfrac{-1}{x(x+h)}\).
  4. Let \(h \to 0\): \(\dfrac{-1}{x \cdot x}\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{f'(x) = -\dfrac{1}{x^2}}\)
▶Example 3 — Power rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,(3x^4 - 5x^2 + 7)\)
  1. Differentiate term by term.
  2. \(3x^4 \to 3 \cdot 4x^3 = 12x^3\).
  3. \(-5x^2 \to -5 \cdot 2x = -10x\).
  4. \(7 \to 0\) (constant).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{12x^3 - 10x}\)
▶Example 4 — Rewrite first: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\sqrt{x} + \dfrac{1}{x^2}\right)\)
  1. Rewrite as powers: \(\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}\) and \(\dfrac{1}{x^2} = x^{-2}\).
  2. Power rule on each: \(\tfrac{1}{2}x^{-1/2}\) and \(-2x^{-3}\).
  3. Rewrite back in radical/fraction form.
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} - \dfrac{2}{x^3}}\)
▶Example 5 — Product rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,(x^2 \sin x)\)
  1. Let \(f = x^2\) and \(g = \sin x\), so \(f' = 2x\) and \(g' = \cos x\).
  2. Apply \((fg)' = f'g + fg'\): \((2x)(\sin x) + (x^2)(\cos x)\).
  3. Answer: \(\;\boxed{2x\sin x + x^2\cos x}\)
▶Example 6 — Quotient rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\dfrac{x^2}{x+1}\)
  1. Let \(f = x^2\) (\(f' = 2x\)) and \(g = x+1\) (\(g' = 1\)).
  2. Apply \(\dfrac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}\): \(\dfrac{(2x)(x+1) - (x^2)(1)}{(x+1)^2}\).
  3. Expand the top: \(2x^2 + 2x - x^2 = x^2 + 2x\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{x^2 + 2x}{(x+1)^2}}\)
▶Example 7 — Deriving \(\tfrac{d}{dx}\tan x\) with the quotient rule
  1. Write \(\tan x = \dfrac{\sin x}{\cos x}\), so \(f = \sin x,\ g = \cos x\).
  2. Quotient rule: \(\dfrac{(\cos x)(\cos x) - (\sin x)(-\sin x)}{\cos^2 x}\).
  3. Top becomes \(\cos^2 x + \sin^2 x = 1\).
  4. So the result is \(\dfrac{1}{\cos^2 x}\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\sec^2 x}\)
▶Example 8 — Tangent line: \(f(x) = x^2\) at \(x = 1\)
  1. \(f'(x) = 2x\), so the slope at \(x = 1\) is \(f'(1) = 2\).
  2. The point on the curve is \((1, f(1)) = (1, 1)\).
  3. Point-slope form: \(y - 1 = 2(x - 1)\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{y = 2x - 1}\)

The tangent line just grazes the parabola at \((1, 1)\) with slope 2 — that's exactly what the derivative measures.

▶Example 9 — Where a derivative fails to exist: \(f(x) = |x|\)
  1. \(f(x) = |x|\) is continuous everywhere, including \(x = 0\).
  2. But just left of \(0\) the slope is \(-1\); just right of \(0\) the slope is \(+1\).
  3. The slopes don't match, so the limit defining \(f'(0)\) does not exist.
  4. Conclusion: \(f\) is \(\;\boxed{\text{not differentiable at } x = 0}\) — a corner.

Continuous but with a sharp corner at the origin: there's no single tangent slope there.
