Composite, Implicit & Inverse Differentiation

Real functions are usually built by nesting, twisting, and inverting simpler ones. The chain rule unlocks all of it — then implicit differentiation and inverse derivatives extend it to curves and inverse functions.

1. The chain rule

For a composite function \(f(g(x))\) — a function inside a function — differentiate the outside, then multiply by the derivative of the inside:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\,f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

Slogan: "derivative of the outside (leave the inside alone), times the derivative of the inside." The "times the derivative of the inside" is the step everyone forgets.

2. Chain rule with the common functions

Memorize these patterns where \(u = u(x)\) is "the inside":

  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,u^n = n\,u^{n-1}\,u'\)
  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,e^{u} = e^{u}\,u'\)
  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\ln u = \dfrac{u'}{u}\)
  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\sin u = \cos u \cdot u'\)

3. Nested chain rule

If something is nested two layers deep, peel one layer at a time, multiplying each derivative as you go inward — like \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,f\big(g(h(x))\big) = f'(g(h(x)))\cdot g'(h(x))\cdot h'(x)\).

4. Implicit differentiation

When \(y\) isn't solved for (like \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\)), differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\), treating \(y\) as a function of \(x\). Every time you differentiate a \(y\), tack on \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) (the chain rule at work). Then solve for \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\).

5. Tangent lines on implicit curves

Once you have \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) as an expression in \(x\) and \(y\), plug in both coordinates of the point to get the slope there, then use point-slope form for the tangent line.

6. Derivatives of inverse functions

An inverse function's slope is the reciprocal of the original's slope (at the matching point):

$$\big(f^{-1}\big)'(x) = \frac{1}{f'\!\big(f^{-1}(x)\big)}$$

In plain terms: if \(f\) sends \(a\mapsto b\) with slope \(m\), then \(f^{-1}\) sends \(b\mapsto a\) with slope \(\tfrac{1}{m}\).

7. Inverse-trig derivatives

  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\arcsin x = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}\)
  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\arccos x = -\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}\)
  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\arctan x = \dfrac{1}{1 + x^2}\)

Combine these with the chain rule when there's an inside function.

▶Example 1 — Chain rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,(3x^2 + 1)^5\)
  1. Outside function: something\(^5\); inside: \(3x^2 + 1\).
  2. Derivative of the outside (leave inside alone): \(5(3x^2 + 1)^4\).
  3. Times the derivative of the inside \((3x^2 + 1)' = 6x\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{30x(3x^2 + 1)^4}\)
▶Example 2 — Chain rule with trig & exponentials
  1. \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\sin(5x)\): outside \(\sin\), inside \(5x\) → \(\cos(5x)\cdot 5 = \boxed{5\cos(5x)}\).
  2. \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,e^{x^2}\): outside \(e^{(\cdot)}\), inside \(x^2\) → \(e^{x^2}\cdot 2x = \boxed{2x\,e^{x^2}}\).
  3. \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\ln(3x)\): \(\dfrac{(3x)'}{3x} = \dfrac{3}{3x} = \boxed{\dfrac{1}{x}}\).
▶Example 3 — Nested chain rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\sqrt{\sin(x^2)}\)
  1. Three layers: square-root (outer), \(\sin\) (middle), \(x^2\) (inner). Rewrite as \(\big(\sin(x^2)\big)^{1/2}\).
  2. Outer: \(\tfrac{1}{2}\big(\sin(x^2)\big)^{-1/2}\).
  3. Times middle \(\cos(x^2)\), times inner \(2x\).
  4. Combine: \(\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{\sin(x^2)}}\cdot \cos(x^2)\cdot 2x\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{x\cos(x^2)}{\sqrt{\sin(x^2)}}}\)
▶Example 4 — Implicit differentiation: \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\)
  1. Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\): \(\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^2) + \dfrac{d}{dx}(y^2) = \dfrac{d}{dx}(25)\).
  2. This gives \(2x + 2y\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 0\) (note the \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) on the \(y\) term).
  3. Solve: \(2y\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -2x\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{x}{y}}\)

This is the circle of radius 5. At the point \((3, 4)\) the slope is \(-\tfrac{3}{4}\) — the tangent line shown.

▶Example 5 — Implicit with a product term: \(x^2 + xy + y^2 = 7\)
  1. Differentiate term by term. The \(xy\) term needs the product rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}(xy) = y + x\dfrac{dy}{dx}\).
  2. Full equation: \(2x + \left(y + x\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right) + 2y\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 0\).
  3. Group the \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) terms: \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\,(x + 2y) = -(2x + y)\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{2x + y}{x + 2y}}\)
▶Example 6 — Inverse trig + chain rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\arctan(x^2)\)
  1. Outside: \(\arctan(\text{inside})\), whose derivative is \(\dfrac{1}{1 + (\text{inside})^2}\).
  2. With inside \(= x^2\): \(\dfrac{1}{1 + (x^2)^2} = \dfrac{1}{1 + x^4}\).
  3. Times the derivative of the inside \((x^2)' = 2x\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{2x}{1 + x^4}}\)
▶Example 7 — Derivative of an inverse at a point

Suppose \(f\) is invertible with \(f(3) = 5\) and \(f'(3) = 2\). Find \(\big(f^{-1}\big)'(5)\).

  1. Use \(\big(f^{-1}\big)'(x) = \dfrac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(x))}\) with \(x = 5\).
  2. Since \(f(3) = 5\), we have \(f^{-1}(5) = 3\).
  3. So \(\big(f^{-1}\big)'(5) = \dfrac{1}{f'(3)} = \dfrac{1}{2}\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\tfrac{1}{2}}\) — the reciprocal of the original slope.
▶Example 8 — Inverses as reflections: \(y = x^2\) and \(y = \sqrt{x}\)
  1. For \(x \ge 0\), \(f(x) = x^2\) and \(f^{-1}(x) = \sqrt{x}\) are inverses.
  2. Their graphs are mirror images across the line \(y = x\).
  3. At \((2, 4)\) on \(x^2\) the slope is \(f'(2) = 4\); at the mirrored point \((4, 2)\) on \(\sqrt{x}\) the slope is \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) — reciprocals, just as the formula promises.
