Composite, Implicit & Inverse Differentiation

1. The chain rule

For a composite function \(f(g(x))\) — a function inside a function — differentiate the outside, then multiply by the derivative of the inside:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\,f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

Slogan: "derivative of the outside (leave the inside alone), times the derivative of the inside."

2. Implicit differentiation

When \(y\) isn't solved for (like \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\)), differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\), treating \(y\) as a function of \(x\). Every time you differentiate a \(y\), tack on \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) (the chain rule at work). Then solve for \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\).

3. Inverse & inverse-trig derivatives

The derivative of an inverse function is the reciprocal of the original's derivative:

$$\big(f^{-1}\big)'(x) = \frac{1}{f'\!\big(f^{-1}(x)\big)}$$

Two to memorize:

  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\arcsin x = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}\)
  • \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\arctan x = \dfrac{1}{1 + x^2}\)

▶Example 1 — Chain rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,(3x^2 + 1)^5\)
  1. Outside function: something\(^5\); inside: \(3x^2 + 1\).
  2. Derivative of the outside (leave inside alone): \(5(3x^2 + 1)^4\).
  3. Times the derivative of the inside \((3x^2 + 1)' = 6x\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{30x(3x^2 + 1)^4}\)
▶Example 2 — Implicit differentiation: \(x^2 + y^2 = 25\)
  1. Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\): \(\dfrac{d}{dx}(x^2) + \dfrac{d}{dx}(y^2) = \dfrac{d}{dx}(25)\).
  2. This gives \(2x + 2y\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 0\) (note the \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) on the \(y\) term).
  3. Solve: \(2y\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -2x\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{x}{y}}\)

This is the circle of radius 5. At the point \((3, 4)\) the slope is \(-\tfrac{3}{4}\) — the tangent line shown.

▶Example 3 — Inverse trig + chain rule: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\,\arctan(x^2)\)
  1. Outside: \(\arctan(\text{inside})\), whose derivative is \(\dfrac{1}{1 + (\text{inside})^2}\).
  2. With inside \(= x^2\): \(\dfrac{1}{1 + (x^2)^2} = \dfrac{1}{1 + x^4}\).
  3. Times the derivative of the inside \((x^2)' = 2x\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{2x}{1 + x^4}}\)
