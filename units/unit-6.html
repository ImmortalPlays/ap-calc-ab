Unit 6: Integration & Accumulation of Change

1. Antiderivatives & the indefinite integral

Integration undoes differentiation. The power rule for integrals:

$$\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \neq -1)$$

The \(+C\) matters — any constant disappears when you differentiate, so we add it back.

2. The definite integral as area

\(\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) is the signed area between the curve and the \(x\)-axis from \(a\) to \(b\). It’s the limit of Riemann sums — rectangles whose widths shrink to zero.

3. Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

It links derivatives and integrals:

$$\textbf{Part 1: }\;\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt = f(x)$$

$$\textbf{Part 2: }\;\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

where \(F\) is any antiderivative of \(f\).

4. \(u\)-substitution

The reverse of the chain rule. Pick an inside function \(u\), compute \(du\), and rewrite the whole integral in terms of \(u\).

Example 1 — Indefinite integral: \(\displaystyle\int (3x^2 + 2x)\,dx\)
  1. Integrate term by term using the power rule.
  2. \(\int 3x^2\,dx = 3 \cdot \dfrac{x^3}{3} = x^3\).
  3. \(\int 2x\,dx = 2 \cdot \dfrac{x^2}{2} = x^2\).
  4. Add the constant of integration.
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{x^3 + x^2 + C}\)
Example 2 — Definite integral via FTC: \(\displaystyle\int_0^2 3x^2\,dx\)
  1. Find an antiderivative: \(F(x) = x^3\).
  2. Apply Part 2: \(F(2) - F(0)\).
  3. \(= 2^3 - 0^3 = 8 - 0\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{8}\) — the area under \(y = 3x^2\) from 0 to 2.

The shaded-looking region under \(y = 3x^2\) between the lines \(x = 0\) and \(x = 2\) has area 8.

Example 3 — \(u\)-substitution: \(\displaystyle\int 2x\,(x^2 + 1)^3\,dx\)
  1. Let \(u = x^2 + 1\). Then \(du = 2x\,dx\) — which is exactly the rest of the integrand.
  2. Rewrite: \(\int u^3\,du\).
  3. Integrate: \(\dfrac{u^4}{4} + C\).
  4. Substitute back \(u = x^2 + 1\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{(x^2 + 1)^4}{4} + C}\)
