UNIT 410–15% of the exam

Contextual Applications of Differentiation

This unit is where derivatives meet the real world: motion, related rates, and approximation. The key skill is reading a word problem and knowing which derivative tool it's asking for.

1. Motion along a line

If \(s(t)\) is position, then velocity and acceleration are its derivatives:

$$v(t) = s'(t), \qquad a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t)$$

Speed is \(|v(t)|\). The object is at rest when \(v(t) = 0\), moving right/up when \(v > 0\) and left/down when \(v < 0\).

2. Speeding up vs. slowing down

This trips everyone up. Compare the signs of velocity and acceleration:

  • Same sign (both \(+\) or both \(-\)) → speeding up.
  • Opposite signs → slowing down.

It's not about whether \(a\) is positive — it's about whether \(v\) and \(a\) agree.

3. Rates of change in context

A derivative is a rate, and on the AP exam units and meaning matter. If \(V(t)\) is volume in liters and \(t\) is in minutes, then \(V'(5) = 3\) means "the volume is increasing at \(3\) liters per minute when \(t = 5\)." Always state a number, a unit, and a time.

4. Related rates

When several quantities change together over time, differentiate the equation relating them with respect to \(t\), then plug in the known rates. The recipe:

  1. Write an equation relating the quantities.
  2. Differentiate both sides with respect to \(t\) (chain rule — every variable gets a \(\frac{d}{dt}\)).
  3. Substitute the known values and rates.
  4. Solve for the unknown rate.

5. L'Hôpital's Rule

If a limit gives the indeterminate form \(\dfrac{0}{0}\) or \(\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}\), you may differentiate top and bottom separately:

$$\lim_{x \to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}$$

Only for those two indeterminate forms — check the form first.

6. Linear approximation

Near \(x = a\), the tangent line is a good stand-in for the curve:

$$L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a)$$

It's how you estimate awkward values (like \(\sqrt{4.1}\)) by hand using a nearby nice point.

7. Geometry formulas for related rates

Related-rates problems almost always lean on a shape formula. Have these ready:

  • Sphere: \(V = \dfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3\)  (surface area \(4\pi r^2\))
  • Cone: \(V = \dfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h\)
  • Cylinder: \(V = \pi r^2 h\)
  • Right triangle / ladder: \(x^2 + y^2 = (\text{hyp})^2\) (Pythagoras)

For a cone, the radius and height usually stay in a fixed ratio — use similar triangles to write one in terms of the other before differentiating.

▶Example 1 — Motion: \(s(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t\)
  1. Velocity: \(v(t) = s'(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9\).
  2. Acceleration: \(a(t) = v'(t) = 6t - 12\).
  3. At rest when \(v(t) = 0\): \(3t^2 - 12t + 9 = 3(t-1)(t-3) = 0\).
  4. Answer: the object is at rest at \(\;\boxed{t = 1 \text{ and } t = 3}\).

The position graph levels off (slope 0) exactly at \(t = 1\) and \(t = 3\) — its local max and local min.

▶Example 2 — Speeding up or slowing down? (same \(s(t)\))
  1. Recall \(v(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9\) and \(a(t) = 6t - 12\) (so \(a = 0\) at \(t = 2\)).
  2. At \(t = 1.5\): \(v = 3(2.25) - 18 + 9 = -2.25\) (\(-\)) and \(a = 9 - 12 = -3\) (\(-\)). Same sign → speeding up.
  3. At \(t = 2.5\): \(v = 3(6.25) - 30 + 9 = -2.25\) (\(-\)) and \(a = 15 - 12 = 3\) (\(+\)). Opposite signs → slowing down.
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\text{speeding up at } t=1.5,\ \text{slowing down at } t=2.5}\)
▶Example 3 — Related rates: an inflating spherical balloon

Air is pumped in at \(\dfrac{dV}{dt} = 100\ \text{cm}^3/\text{s}\). How fast is the radius growing when \(r = 5\) cm?

  1. Relationship: \(V = \dfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3\).
  2. Differentiate with respect to \(t\): \(\dfrac{dV}{dt} = 4\pi r^2 \dfrac{dr}{dt}\).
  3. Substitute \(\dfrac{dV}{dt} = 100\) and \(r = 5\): \(100 = 4\pi(25)\dfrac{dr}{dt}\).
  4. Solve: \(\dfrac{dr}{dt} = \dfrac{100}{100\pi}\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{dr}{dt} = \dfrac{1}{\pi}\ \text{cm/s}}\)
▶Example 4 — Related rates: a sliding ladder

A 10-ft ladder leans on a wall. The base slides away at \(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = 1\) ft/s. How fast is the top sliding down when the base is \(6\) ft from the wall?

  1. Pythagoras relates base \(x\) and height \(y\): \(x^2 + y^2 = 100\).
  2. When \(x = 6\): \(y = \sqrt{100 - 36} = 8\).
  3. Differentiate in \(t\): \(2x\dfrac{dx}{dt} + 2y\dfrac{dy}{dt} = 0\).
  4. Substitute: \(2(6)(1) + 2(8)\dfrac{dy}{dt} = 0 \Rightarrow 12 + 16\dfrac{dy}{dt} = 0\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{dy}{dt} = -\dfrac{3}{4}\ \text{ft/s}}\) (negative = sliding down).
▶Example 5 — L'Hôpital (form \(\tfrac{0}{0}\)): \(\lim_{x \to 0}\dfrac{\sin x}{x}\)
  1. Direct substitution gives \(\dfrac{\sin 0}{0} = \dfrac{0}{0}\) — indeterminate, so L'Hôpital applies.
  2. Differentiate top and bottom separately: \(\dfrac{(\sin x)'}{(x)'} = \dfrac{\cos x}{1}\).
  3. Now substitute \(x = 0\): \(\dfrac{\cos 0}{1} = \dfrac{1}{1}\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{1}\)
▶Example 6 — L'Hôpital (form \(\tfrac{\infty}{\infty}\)): \(\lim_{x \to \infty}\dfrac{\ln x}{x}\)
  1. As \(x \to \infty\), top and bottom both \(\to \infty\): form \(\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}\).
  2. Differentiate separately: \(\dfrac{(\ln x)'}{(x)'} = \dfrac{1/x}{1} = \dfrac{1}{x}\).
  3. As \(x \to \infty\), \(\dfrac{1}{x} \to 0\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{0}\) — \(x\) grows much faster than \(\ln x\).
▶Example 7 — Linear approximation: estimate \(\sqrt{4.1}\)
  1. Use \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\) at the nearby nice point \(a = 4\), where \(f(4) = 2\).
  2. \(f'(x) = \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\), so \(f'(4) = \dfrac{1}{4}\).
  3. Tangent line: \(L(x) = 2 + \tfrac{1}{4}(x - 4)\).
  4. Plug in \(x = 4.1\): \(L(4.1) = 2 + \tfrac{1}{4}(0.1) = 2.025\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\sqrt{4.1} \approx 2.025}\) (true value \(2.0248\dots\) — very close).

Near \(x = 4\) the tangent line hugs the curve, so reading the line gives an excellent estimate.

▶Example 8 — Related rates: a draining cone

Water drains from an inverted cone (point down) of height \(12\) and top radius \(6\) at \(\dfrac{dV}{dt} = -3\ \text{ft}^3/\text{min}\). How fast is the water level dropping when the depth is \(h = 4\)?

  1. Similar triangles fix the ratio: \(\dfrac{r}{h} = \dfrac{6}{12} = \dfrac{1}{2}\), so \(r = \dfrac{h}{2}\).
  2. Write \(V\) in terms of \(h\) only: \(V = \dfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h = \dfrac{1}{3}\pi\left(\dfrac{h}{2}\right)^2 h = \dfrac{\pi h^3}{12}\).
  3. Differentiate in \(t\): \(\dfrac{dV}{dt} = \dfrac{\pi h^2}{4}\dfrac{dh}{dt}\).
  4. Substitute \(\dfrac{dV}{dt} = -3\), \(h = 4\): \(-3 = \dfrac{\pi(16)}{4}\dfrac{dh}{dt} = 4\pi\dfrac{dh}{dt}\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{dh}{dt} = -\dfrac{3}{4\pi}\ \text{ft/min}}\) (negative = dropping).
