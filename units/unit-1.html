Limits & Continuity

Limits are the foundation everything else in calculus is built on. This unit takes you from the intuitive idea all the way to continuity and the Intermediate Value Theorem — with worked examples and graphs you can drag for every idea.

1. What is a limit?

A limit describes the value a function approaches as the input gets close to some number. We write:

$$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$$

and read it "as \(x\) gets close to \(a\), \(f(x)\) gets close to \(L\)." The single most important idea: a limit is about what happens near \(a\), not what happens at \(a\). The function might not even be defined at \(a\) — the limit can still exist.

2. Estimating limits from tables & graphs

Before any algebra, you can estimate a limit two ways:

  • From a table: plug in \(x\)-values creeping toward \(a\) from both sides and watch where \(f(x)\) heads.
  • From a graph: trace the curve toward \(x = a\) from the left and from the right and see what height it aims for.

If both sides aim for the same height, that height is the limit — even if there's a hole or a different dot exactly at \(a\).

3. One-sided limits & when a limit fails to exist

You can approach \(a\) from one side at a time:

  • Left-hand limit (from below \(a\)): \(\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x)\)
  • Right-hand limit (from above \(a\)): \(\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\)

The two-sided limit exists only if both sides agree:

$$\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L$$

A limit does not exist (DNE) when: the two sides disagree (a jump), the function blows up to \(\pm\infty\), or it oscillates forever without settling.

4. The limit laws

If \(\lim_{x\to a} f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x\to a} g(x)\) both exist, limits behave exactly how you'd hope — they split across sums, products, and quotients:

  • \(\lim (f \pm g) = \lim f \pm \lim g\)
  • \(\lim (f \cdot g) = \lim f \cdot \lim g\)
  • \(\lim \dfrac{f}{g} = \dfrac{\lim f}{\lim g}\)  (as long as \(\lim g \neq 0\))
  • \(\lim \big[c\,f\big] = c\,\lim f\) for any constant \(c\)

These are what justify "just plug the number in" for nice functions.

5. Evaluating limits algebraically

Always try direct substitution first. If you get a real number, you're done. If you get the indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\), the function needs rewriting before it will cooperate:

  • Factor & cancel when top and bottom are polynomials.
  • Rationalize (multiply by the conjugate) when there's a square root.
  • Combine fractions when you have a complex fraction.

The form \(\frac{0}{0}\) is "indeterminate" — it doesn't mean DNE, it means do more work.

6. Special trig limits & the Squeeze Theorem

Two trig limits are worth memorizing cold:

$$\lim_{x \to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x} = 1, \qquad \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{1 - \cos x}{x} = 0$$

The Squeeze (Sandwich) Theorem handles functions trapped between two others: if \(g(x) \le f(x) \le h(x)\) near \(a\) and \(\lim_{x\to a} g = \lim_{x\to a} h = L\), then \(\lim_{x\to a} f = L\) too. It's the classic tool for wild oscillating functions.

7. Infinite limits & limits at infinity

  • Infinite limit: the function blows up near a point (like \(\frac{1}{x-2}\) at \(x = 2\)). The limit is \(\pm\infty\) and you get a vertical asymptote.
  • Limit at infinity: \(x\) itself grows without bound. \(\lim_{x \to \pm\infty} f(x)\) describes the end behavior, often a horizontal asymptote.

Shortcut for rational functions \(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\) as \(x\to\pm\infty\): compare degrees. Bottom bigger → limit \(0\); degrees equal → ratio of leading coefficients; top bigger → \(\pm\infty\).

8. Continuity at a point

A function is continuous at \(x = a\) if all three are true:

  1. \(f(a)\) is defined
  2. \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists
  3. \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\)

If any one fails there's a discontinuity. The three types: removable (a hole), jump, and infinite (a vertical asymptote). "Continuous" intuitively means you can draw it without lifting your pencil.

9. The Intermediate Value Theorem

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and \(N\) is any value between \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\), then \(f\) hits \(N\) at least once in \((a, b)\).

In practice it's how you prove a solution exists: if \(f\) is continuous and changes sign across an interval, there must be a root in between.

▶Example 1 — Direct substitution: \(\lim_{x \to 3}\,(2x^2 - 4x + 1)\)
  1. This is a polynomial, continuous everywhere, so substitute directly.
  2. Plug in \(x = 3\): \(\;2(3)^2 - 4(3) + 1\)
  3. Simplify: \(\;18 - 12 + 1\)
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{7}\)
▶Example 2 — Estimating from a table: \(\lim_{x \to 1}\dfrac{x^3 - 1}{x - 1}\)

Direct substitution gives \(\frac{0}{0}\), so build a table closing in on \(x = 1\) from both sides:

  1. \(x = 0.9 \Rightarrow 2.710\);   \(x = 0.99 \Rightarrow 2.970\);   \(x = 0.999 \Rightarrow 2.997\)
  2. \(x = 1.1 \Rightarrow 3.310\);   \(x = 1.01 \Rightarrow 3.030\);   \(x = 1.001 \Rightarrow 3.003\)
  3. Both sides march toward the same value.
  4. Estimate: \(\;\boxed{3}\)  (and factoring \(x^3-1=(x-1)(x^2+x+1)\) confirms it exactly).

On a TI-84 you'd build this with 2ND → GRAPH (TABLE) — see the Calculator Guide.

▶Example 3 — Factoring a \(\tfrac{0}{0}\) form: \(\lim_{x \to 2}\dfrac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}\)
  1. Direct substitution: \(\dfrac{2^2 - 4}{2 - 2} = \dfrac{0}{0}\) — indeterminate, so rewrite.
  2. Factor the numerator: \(x^2 - 4 = (x - 2)(x + 2)\).
  3. Cancel the common \((x - 2)\): \(\dfrac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = x + 2\).
  4. Substitute \(x = 2\): \(\;2 + 2\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{4}\)

The graph is the line \(y = x + 2\) with an open hole at \((2, 4)\) — the limit is \(4\) even though the function isn't defined there.

▶Example 4 — Rationalizing: \(\lim_{x \to 0}\dfrac{\sqrt{x+4} - 2}{x}\)
  1. Direct substitution gives \(\dfrac{\sqrt{4} - 2}{0} = \dfrac{0}{0}\) — indeterminate.
  2. Multiply top and bottom by the conjugate \(\sqrt{x+4} + 2\):
  3. $$\dfrac{\sqrt{x+4}-2}{x}\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{x+4}+2}{\sqrt{x+4}+2} = \dfrac{(x+4) - 4}{x\left(\sqrt{x+4}+2\right)}$$
  4. Simplify and cancel: \(\dfrac{x}{x(\sqrt{x+4}+2)} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x+4}+2}\).
  5. Substitute \(x = 0\): \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{4}+2} = \dfrac{1}{4}\).
  6. Answer: \(\;\boxed{\tfrac{1}{4}}\)
▶Example 5 — A complex fraction: \(\lim_{x \to 0}\dfrac{\frac{1}{x+2} - \frac{1}{2}}{x}\)
  1. Substitution gives \(\frac{0}{0}\). First combine the top over a common denominator \(2(x+2)\):
  2. $$\frac{1}{x+2} - \frac{1}{2} = \frac{2 - (x+2)}{2(x+2)} = \frac{-x}{2(x+2)}$$
  3. Now divide by \(x\) (multiply by \(\frac{1}{x}\)): \(\dfrac{-x}{2(x+2)}\cdot\dfrac{1}{x} = \dfrac{-1}{2(x+2)}\).
  4. Substitute \(x = 0\): \(\dfrac{-1}{2(2)}\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{-\tfrac{1}{4}}\)
▶Example 6 — One-sided limits & a jump: \(f(x)=\begin{cases}x^2 & x<1\\ x+1 & x\ge 1\end{cases}\)
  1. From the left (use \(x^2\)): \(\lim_{x \to 1^-} f(x) = 1^2 = 1\).
  2. From the right (use \(x+1\)): \(\lim_{x \to 1^+} f(x) = 1 + 1 = 2\).
  3. The two sides disagree: \(1 \neq 2\).
  4. Conclusion: \(\lim_{x \to 1} f(x)\) does not exist — a jump discontinuity.

Open circle at \((1, 1)\) where the left piece stops; filled dot at \((1, 2)\) where the right piece starts. The graph literally jumps.

▶Example 7 — A limit that DNE: \(\lim_{x \to 0}\dfrac{|x|}{x}\)
  1. For \(x > 0\), \(|x| = x\), so \(\dfrac{|x|}{x} = 1\): the right-hand limit is \(1\).
  2. For \(x < 0\), \(|x| = -x\), so \(\dfrac{|x|}{x} = -1\): the left-hand limit is \(-1\).
  3. The sides disagree (\(-1 \neq 1\)).
  4. Answer: the limit \(\;\boxed{\text{does not exist}}\).

The graph is two flat rays — height \(+1\) for positive \(x\), height \(-1\) for negative \(x\) — with a break at the origin.

▶Example 8 — Squeeze Theorem: \(\lim_{x \to 0} x^2 \cos\!\left(\tfrac{1}{x}\right)\)
  1. \(\cos\) of anything stays between \(-1\) and \(1\): \(-1 \le \cos\!\left(\tfrac{1}{x}\right) \le 1\).
  2. Multiply through by \(x^2\) (never negative): \(-x^2 \le x^2\cos\!\left(\tfrac{1}{x}\right) \le x^2\).
  3. As \(x \to 0\), both \(-x^2 \to 0\) and \(x^2 \to 0\).
  4. Squeezed between two things going to \(0\): \(\;\boxed{0}\).

The middle curve wiggles infinitely fast near \(0\) but is trapped inside the two parabolas \(y = \pm x^2\), which pinch it to the origin.

▶Example 9 — The famous trig limit: \(\lim_{x \to 0}\dfrac{\sin x}{x}\)
  1. Substitution gives \(\frac{0}{0}\) — but this is a known special limit.
  2. The table backs it up: \(\frac{\sin(0.1)}{0.1} \approx 0.9983\), \(\frac{\sin(0.01)}{0.01} \approx 0.99998\).
  3. Both sides approach the same height.
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{1}\). (Memorize this — it powers the derivative of \(\sin x\).)

Notice the curve has a hole at \(x = 0\) but clearly aims for height \(1\) there.

▶Example 10 — Infinite limit: \(\lim_{x \to 2}\dfrac{1}{x-2}\)
  1. Substituting gives \(\dfrac{1}{0}\) — undefined, so check each side.
  2. From the left (\(x\) just below 2): bottom is a tiny negative, so \(\lim_{x \to 2^-}\dfrac{1}{x-2} = -\infty\).
  3. From the right (\(x\) just above 2): bottom is a tiny positive, so \(\lim_{x \to 2^+}\dfrac{1}{x-2} = +\infty\).
  4. Opposite infinities, so the two-sided limit does not exist. Vertical asymptote at \(x = 2\).

Watch the curve dive to \(-\infty\) just left of the dashed line and rocket to \(+\infty\) just right of it.

▶Example 11 — Limit at infinity (rational): \(\lim_{x \to \infty}\dfrac{3x^2 + 2}{x^2 + 1}\)
  1. Same degree on top and bottom (both \(x^2\)), so compare leading coefficients.
  2. Divide every term by \(x^2\): \(\dfrac{3 + 2/x^2}{1 + 1/x^2}\).
  3. As \(x \to \infty\), \(2/x^2\) and \(1/x^2\) shrink to \(0\): \(\dfrac{3 + 0}{1 + 0}\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{3}\). The line \(y = 3\) is a horizontal asymptote.

Zoom out: the curve flattens toward the dashed line \(y = 3\) in both directions.

▶Example 12 — Limit at infinity with a root: \(\lim_{x \to \infty}\dfrac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 + 1}}\)
  1. For large \(x\), \(\sqrt{x^2+1}\) behaves like \(\sqrt{x^2} = |x| = x\).
  2. Divide top and bottom by \(x\) (using \(x = \sqrt{x^2}\) inside the root): \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1 + 1/x^2}}\).
  3. As \(x \to \infty\), \(1/x^2 \to 0\): \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1}} = 1\).
  4. Answer: \(\;\boxed{1}\). (And \(\lim_{x\to-\infty} = -1\) — a second horizontal asymptote.)

The curve levels off toward \(y = 1\) on the right and \(y = -1\) on the left.

▶Example 13 — Checking continuity at a point

Let \(f(x) = \dfrac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}\) for \(x \neq 1\), and \(f(1) = 3\). Is \(f\) continuous at \(x = 1\)?

  1. Is \(f(1)\) defined? Yes, \(f(1) = 3\).
  2. Does the limit exist? Factor: \(\dfrac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1} = x + 1\), so \(\lim_{x \to 1} f(x) = 2\).
  3. Do they match? The limit is \(2\) but \(f(1) = 3\), so \(2 \neq 3\).
  4. Conclusion: not continuous at \(x = 1\) — a removable discontinuity (a hole).

The open hole sits at \((1, 2)\) where the curve naturally goes; the red dot is the separately-defined \(f(1) = 3\) floating above it.

▶Example 14 — Make a piecewise function continuous

Find \(a\) so that \(f(x)=\begin{cases}x^2 & x \le 2\\ ax + 1 & x > 2\end{cases}\) is continuous at \(x = 2\).

  1. For continuity the two pieces must meet: \(\lim_{x\to 2^-} = \lim_{x\to 2^+} = f(2)\).
  2. Left piece at \(2\): \(2^2 = 4\).
  3. Right piece at \(2\): \(a(2) + 1 = 2a + 1\).
  4. Set them equal: \(2a + 1 = 4 \Rightarrow 2a = 3\).
  5. Answer: \(\;\boxed{a = \tfrac{3}{2}}\)
▶Example 15 — Intermediate Value Theorem: does \(x^3 + x - 1 = 0\) have a solution in \([0,1]\)?
  1. \(f(x) = x^3 + x - 1\) is a polynomial, so it's continuous on \([0, 1]\).
  2. Endpoints: \(f(0) = -1\) (negative) and \(f(1) = 1\) (positive).
  3. \(0\) lies between \(-1\) and \(1\), and \(f\) is continuous.
  4. By the IVT there is some \(c\) in \((0, 1)\) with \(\;\boxed{f(c) = 0}\) — a guaranteed root.

The curve starts below the \(x\)-axis at \(x=0\) and ends above it at \(x=1\), so it must cross somewhere in between (near \(x \approx 0.68\)).
